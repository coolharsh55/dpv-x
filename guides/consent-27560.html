This document provides guidance on the implementation of ISO/IEC TR 27560:2023 Consent Records using the Data Privacy Vocabulary (DPV).

Additionally, this document also provides guidance on the implementation of [[27560]] in a manner that supports the obligations and requirements of GDPR compliance.

This document is published by the Data Privacy Vocabularies and Controls Community Group (DPVCG) as a deliverable and report of its work in creating and maintaining the Data Privacy Vocabulary (DPV).

Contributing to the DPV and its extensions The DPVCG welcomes participation regarding the DPV, including expansion or refinement of its terms, addressing open issues, and welcomes suggestions on their resolution or mitigation. For further information, please see the contribution section.

Introduction

Lifecycle of Consent

Processing of personal data utilises Consent as one of the possible legal basis. In the current regulatory environment, such consent has to be valid according to several criterias such as informed, freely given, or unambigious. For consent to be informed, the data subject must have been provided or be aware of information, typically through a Consent Notice, describing the effect of their consent i.e. which processing activities will take place, over which data, and who is involved in terms of entities. Other criterias, such as being unambigious are determined through the manner in which consent is expressed e.g. the clicking of a button or a signature on paper.

Consent Records

[[[ISO-27560]]] defines Consent Record as the documentation of information about a data subject's consent for the processing of their personal data in terms of the details about the processing as well as the interactions related to consent (e.g. giving or withdrawing it). Consent Records are an essential part of keeping records regarding whether consent has been obtained and is valid for processing, and to keep this information for correctly conducting processing relying on it. [[ISO-27560]] as well as regulatory requirements such as GDPR Article 7-1 require maintaining of consent records where consent is used as the legal basis. While GDPR Article 7-1 only states that consent should be demonstrable, [[ISO-27560]] provides an information structure for how this information should be maintained and what processes should exist within an organisation in connection with it.

It is important to distinguish between a Consent Record with several relevant but distinct concepts. A consent record only refers to the information recorded regarding consent, whereas a Consent Notice refers to the notice and information provided to the data subject in order to inform them about the processing while requesting consent. While there is a significant overlap between a consent record and a consent notice, there are key differences such as the exact phrasing of information (e.g. description of the purpose) or the manner in which consent was expressed (e.g. clicking a button) - which are not depicted within a consent record. This is as [[[ISO-29184]]] specifies what information should be present in a notice and the manner in which it is presented, which is relied on by [[27560]] to limit its scope for how to represent it as consent records.

Therefore a consent record despite containing a link to the notice is not by itself sufficient to determine the validity of consent, and instead acts as the primary record containing information or links to information for conducting such assessments. Its primary purpose is therefore limited to provide an indication of the state of consent (e.g. request, given, terminated) for specific processing activities (e.g. which purpose, what recipients).

This document provides guidance on the implementation of the ISO/IEC TR 27560:2023 Consent Records using the Data Privacy Vocabulary (DPV). It also defines and describes the DPV-based schema for maintaining [[27560]] conformant consent records. More specifically, this document provides guidance on the maintainence of the following information in a structure representation as per the requirements prescribed by [[27560]:

  1. metadata about the consent record such as its identifier
  2. the individual associated with the consent i.e. data subject
  3. the subject of consent i.e. specifics of the processing of personal data
  4. entities involved e.g. data controller and third parties
  5. relevant contextual information e.g. notice, rights, restrictions
  6. provenance of events associated with the consent e.g. given, withdrawn

In addition to the above, this document also provides guidance on the implementation of [[27560]] to support [[GDPR]] compliance requirements in terms of maintaining information regarding consent. This is described in visually distinct notes within their relevant sections to differentiate between [[27560]] and [[GDPR]] requirements and notes. The GDPR specific content can be hidden through the button provided in abstract.

considerations on security (informative)

  • record is personal data - information within a consent record and the record itself also constitutes personal data, therefore appropriate measures are required to safeguard it
  • identification for record/receipt - each consent must be attributed to the data subject it is about, however this does not mean records and receipts require additional identification procedures to get this information. Where no identity or identifier is available, a new identifier should be created for the purposes of record/receipt only e.g. visitors to the website
  • integrity of contents - mechanisms such as using cryptographic hashes to provide integrity checks for information, ensuring storage retains accuracy of information, and any changes to structure of information should be assessed to ensure interpretation of contents of records/receipts is not changed
  • verification of contents - disputes on changes in contents and discrepancies between records or receipts held by two or more entities are possible; mechanisms to cryptopgraphically 'sign' the information using verifiable means can assist in preventing such issues. The current norm within 27560 is Controllers creating records and providing receipts to the data subjects who can then use it to keep their own records. This guide does not presume this is the only possible use-case, and considers any entity
  • transmission / communication - between controllers
  • communication of receipt to data subjects

consideration on modeling (informative)

  • for controllers - storing full records/receipts is not required
  • for controllers - separate records or single records
  • for controllers - updating vs appending
  • for data subjects - creating own records
  • for data subjects - storing and using receipts

recording privacy signals

  • recording use of signals
  • DNT, GPC
  • DNT (full), P3P, ADPC

  • receipts - A13/A14, signals only, full+explicit i.e. mention not applicable in fields instead of blanks
